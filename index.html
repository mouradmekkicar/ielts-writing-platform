    The exam at a glance

    IELTS Writing exam overview

    60 minutes · two tasks · one focused mind

    You have 60 minutes total for both tasks. There is no extra time, so managing the clock is part of the skill being tested.
    Task 1

    Academic report

    Describe visual information — a graph, chart, table, map, or process — in your own words. At least 150 words.

    • Spend about 20 minutes
    • Report the data; do not give opinions
    • Worth one third of your writing score
    vs
    Task 2

    Essay

    Respond to a question or statement with a clear, well-argued essay. At least 250 words.

    • Spend about 40 minutes
    • Take a position and develop it
    • Worth two thirds of your writing score

    Because Task 2 carries more weight, never let Task 1 eat into your Task 2 time.

    Task 1 explained

    Writing Task 1

    Describing data and processes

    Task 1 gives you one or more visuals. Your job is to select the key information and report it accurately and objectively. You may be asked to describe:

    📈Line graphsTrends and changes over time
    📊Bar chartsComparisons between groups
    🥧Pie chartsProportions of a whole
    ▦TablesExact figures across categories
    🗺️MapsChanges to a place over time
    ⚙️Process diagramsStages of how something works
    🧩Mixed chartsTwo visuals to combine

    What you must do

    • Summarise the main features — the biggest, smallest, fastest, most striking points.
    • Make comparisons rather than listing every number separately.
    • Select key information — you cannot describe everything, so choose what matters.
    • Write a clear overview — one or two sentences capturing the big picture.
    • Avoid personal opinions — report only what the data shows.
    • Use accurate data language — rose, fell, peaked, plateaued, roughly, a quarter of.
    Task 2 explained

    Writing Task 2

    Building a clear argument

    Task 2 asks for an essay responding to a question. First, identify the essay type — it tells you how to structure your answer:

    💬OpinionAgree or disagree, and why
    ⚖️DiscussionBoth views, then your view
    ➕➖Advantages & disadvantagesWeigh the two sides
    🛠️Problem & solutionCauses and practical fixes
    ❓Two-part questionAnswer both parts fully

    What you must do

    • Answer the question directly — address exactly what is asked, not a similar topic.
    • Develop clear ideas — one main idea per paragraph, fully explained.
    • Use examples to support and illustrate your points.
    • Organise paragraphs logically with a clear introduction, body, and conclusion.
    • Maintain a formal academic tone throughout.

    How writing is scored

    The four band descriptors

    What examiners actually reward

    Each task is marked on four equally weighted criteria. Knowing them turns marking from a mystery into a checklist.

    01

    Task Achievement / Task Response

    Did you fully answer the question? Task 1 needs an accurate overview and key features; Task 2 needs a clear position developed throughout.

    Focus on: answering completely and staying on topic.

    02

    Coherence & Cohesion

    Is your writing easy to follow? Logical paragraphs, clear progression, and natural linking words that connect ideas without overuse.

    Focus on: paragraphing and smooth, logical flow.

    03

    Lexical Resource

    Is your vocabulary varied, precise, and used correctly? Range matters, but accuracy and the right word matter more than rare words.

    Focus on: precise word choice and correct collocations.

    04

    Grammatical Range & Accuracy

    Can you use a mix of sentence structures with few errors? A variety of accurate sentences scores higher than long, error-filled ones.

    Focus on: control first, then variety.

    Dos & Don'ts

    ✔ Do

    • Plan before you write.
    • Answer the exact question asked.
    • Write a clear overview in Task 1.
    • Organise your answer into paragraphs.
    • Support your ideas with examples.
    • Check grammar, spelling, and punctuation.
    • Use formal academic English.

    ✕ Don't

    • Memorise whole essays in advance.
    • Copy the question without paraphrasing.
    • Give opinions in Task 1.
    • Ignore or forget the overview.
    • Drift off-topic.
    • Force in complex words you can't use accurately.
    • Use informal language or spend too long on Task 1.
    Success tips

    Task 1

    Task 1 success tips

    • Open with a paraphrased version of the prompt — don't copy it.
    • Always include an overview of the main trends or stages.
    • Group information logically instead of describing at random.
    • Compare figures rather than listing every single number.
    • Use accurate trend vocabulary (rose sharply, levelled off, gradual decline).
    • Don't explain reasons unless the data actually shows them.

    Task 2

    Task 2 success tips

    • Analyse the question carefully before you start.
    • Decide your position first, then build toward it.
    • Write clear topic sentences to open each paragraph.
    • Develop one main idea per paragraph.
    • Use examples naturally, not as decoration.
    • Keep the conclusion short, clear, and on-message.

    Final check

    Writing checklist before submission

    • Did I answer the question?
    • Did I write enough words (150+ / 250+)?
    • Did I organise my answer into paragraphs?
    • Did I include an overview for Task 1?
    • Did I support my Task 2 ideas with reasons or examples?
    • Did I check spelling, grammar, and punctuation?
    • Did I avoid informal language?

    Before you begin

    Write better, not just more

    IELTS Writing improves through strategy, practice, and feedback. The goal is never simply to write more words — it is to write clearer, sharper, more accurate ones.

    Start with a single task. Review the feedback. Improve one thing. Then do it again. That loop, repeated patiently, is how confident writers are made.
